What Tulsa Homeowners Deal With (And How We Fix It)

Tulsa's geography and housing stock create plumbing challenges you won't find anywhere else in Oklahoma:

Red clay and expansive soil. Tulsa sits on soil that swells when wet and shrinks when dry. That constant movement stresses underground pipes and slab foundations year after year, causing joint separations and slab cracks that let water escape silently for months.

Clay sewer pipes in older homes. Homes built before 1970 — much of Midtown, East Tulsa, and North Tulsa — were plumbed with clay tile sewer lines. These crack, root-intrude, and collapse. We inspect and replace them regularly.

Hard water scale. South Tulsa and surrounding areas like Bixby and Jenks deal with hard water that deposits calcium and magnesium inside pipes and water heaters, reducing flow and cutting equipment life in half.

Oak and elm tree roots. Tulsa's tree canopy is beautiful, but oak and elm roots are aggressive. They follow warm sewer lines underground and will fill a 6-inch pipe completely if left unchecked for a season.
